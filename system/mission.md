# Lifehug Mission

Every life is a story worth telling. Lifehug exists to make sure yours gets told well.

## Three Purposes

1. **Help others understand me** — Build a rich, navigable story so that someone reading it could know who I am. The people who shaped me, the places that mattered, the moments that changed everything. Not a resume. A life.

2. **Enable me to tell my story** — Give me the questions, arcs, and outputs so I can produce tangible artifacts: letters, essays, chapters, memoirs, founder stories. Every neighborhood of questions should move toward something I can hold, send, or publish.

3. **Help me understand myself** — Surface patterns, contradictions, philosophy, faith, and self-knowledge I haven't articulated yet. See relationships across people, places, time periods, projects, and themes that aren't obvious from any single answer. The best memoirs aren't chronological — they're organized around turning points and themes. Help me find mine.

## How It Works

One thoughtful question at a time. Voice or text, long or short. A compounding, AI-assisted memory system that gets smarter with every answer — discovering connections, deepening thin areas, and turning raw stories into a living, interlinked wiki of my life.

## For AI Prompts

Every prompt, classification, neighborhood, and planner decision should serve at least one of these three purposes. If a question doesn't help someone understand the author, help the author tell their story, or help the author understand themselves — it's the wrong question.

Questions should draw from proven conversation-starter methodologies — oral history (StoryCorps), vulnerability games (We're Not Really Strangers), philosophical self-examination (The School of Life), escalating intimacy (36 Questions), unexpected angles (Table Topics), therapy frameworks (narrative therapy, CBT reframing), and faith/spirituality prompts. The goal is not just recall — it's discovery.

Neighborhoods are output-oriented arcs. Each one should have a target output (chapter, letter, essay, post) and an arc that builds toward it. When someone finishes a neighborhood, they should have enough material to produce something tangible.

Gap detection matters: thin time periods, missing family members, unexplored themes (faith, philosophy, values) should trigger new questions. The system should notice what's missing, not just what's been said.
